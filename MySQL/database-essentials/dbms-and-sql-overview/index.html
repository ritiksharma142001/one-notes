Types of Data Storage
When setting up a database, think of it as choosing the best way to store different types of items in your home. Data can be stored in various forms, depending on what fits best for your needs.

Tabular Form: This is like storing items in a grid or shelf where everything is lined up in rows and columns. It’s straightforward and organized. Databases like MySQL and Oracle use this tabular form, known as relational databases (RDBMS). They are great for when your data fits well into a structured format, like lists of names, addresses, or any data that can be neatly divided into categories.
For example assume you have an university database and you are trying to store student data in it in the tabular form then the data in the database might look as shown below.

Document Form: Imagine keeping your data in a folder where each piece of information can be a different type of document. This is what document-oriented databases like MongoDB do. They store data in JSON-like documents, which makes them flexible to accommodate various data types without needing a strict structure. This is suitable for when your data doesn’t fit neatly into tables and might have different attributes or fields for each entry.

Graph Form: This form is like a network of connections, similar to a family tree or a map showing how different points connect. Graph databases, like Neo4j, store data in nodes, edges, and properties. They excel in scenarios where relationships between data points are crucial, such as social networks, recommendation systems, or any complex web of connections.

For example, think of a teacher as someone with basic information like a name and last name, which we usually know and don't need to point out specifically. However, it's important to note other details like the languages they can teach. So, in our setup, each teacher will have a unique ID, their first and last name, an email address, their date of birth, and the languages they are capable of teaching. We'll organize similar details for other groups as well. By the end, we'll have a clear setup showing all this information, laid out in an easy-to-understand way.

Setting Up Your Database​

Now that you know about the different forms data can take, setting up your database is like deciding how to organize your living space based on what items you have.
If your data fits well into a structured format of rows and columns, like student records or product inventories, you might choose a relational database management system (RDBMS) like MySQL or Oracle.
If your data varies a lot and each item doesn't necessarily fit into the same format, a document-oriented database like MongoDB might be more suitable.
If you need to manage data that is heavily interconnected, like understanding relationships between customers or analyzing network infrastructures, a graph database could be the best choice.

Filling Your Database​

With the right type of database selected, filling it with data is like placing your items in the organized space you’ve created. Whether it's placing books on shelves, filing documents in folders, or drawing connections on a map, the way you add data depends on the type of database you are using.

Retrieving Your Data Efficiently
Finally, accessing your data efficiently is key to a well-managed database. Depending on your database type, you'll use different methods to retrieve data:
In relational databases, you'll look through rows and columns.
In document databases, you'll search through various documents.
In graph databases, you'll navigate through the connections.
